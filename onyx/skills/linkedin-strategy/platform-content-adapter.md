# Skill: platform-content-adapter

## Purpose
Take a single insight, project, or experience and produce two distinct versions: one for LinkedIn, one for Substack. These are not the same content at different lengths — they are different angles of the same material, built for different purposes.

## Core rule
**LinkedIn generates discovery. Substack generates authority.**
A LinkedIn post that performs well sends people to Substack.
A Substack article that delivers value brings people back to LinkedIn.
They are a system, not duplicates.

## When to invoke
- When Lucía has a project, observation, or lesson worth developing across both channels
- When an existing LinkedIn post has depth that could become a Substack article
- When planning the content for a new phase and wanting to maximize a project's reach

## Adaptation logic

### LinkedIn version
- Format: 150–250 words
- Structure: hook → observation or story → insight → CTA
- Goal: engagement, discovery, "I've never thought about it that way"
- Angle: the surprising observation, the counter-intuitive lesson, the relatable frustration
- Ends with: a question, a prompt to comment, or a pointer to the Substack article
- Tone: sharp, direct, human

### Substack version
- Format: 800–1500 words
- Structure: problem → context → process → what I learned → actionable close
- Goal: depth, trust, "I'm going to bookmark this"
- Angle: the full story, the how, the specific tools and decisions
- Ends with: something the reader can do or think about differently
- Tone: reflective, practical, pedagogical

## Two-angle framework

The same material has multiple valid angles. LinkedIn and Substack should use *different* ones:

| Material | LinkedIn angle | Substack angle |
|----------|----------------|----------------|
| P1 reconciliation app | "Nadie me pidió hacerlo" (motivation) | "Cómo construí la app" (full process) |
| Manual Excel process | "Por qué aún uso Excel para X" (opinion) | "Cuándo Excel es la herramienta correcta" (framework) |
| P2 email automation | "Automaticé el email que mandaba todos los jueves" (result) | "Stack mínimo para email automation en AP" (guide) |
| Month-end close | "Lo que pasa cuando no documentás tu proceso" (risk) | "Cómo documentar antes de automatizar" (method) |

## Input
- Raw material: project description, observation, draft post, or experience note
- Channel priority: which platform to optimize for first (usually LinkedIn)

## Output format

```
---
INPUT MATERIAL: [summary of source]
---

## LINKEDIN VERSION
**Positioning angle:** [what this does for Lucía's brand]
**Hook:** [opening line]
**Full post:**
[complete LinkedIn post — 150–250 words]
**CTA:** [suggested call to action]

---

## SUBSTACK VERSION
**Article angle:** [different from LinkedIn — why it's complementary, not redundant]
**Title:** [article title]
**Outline:**
- [Section 1]
- [Section 2]
- [Section 3]
- [What I learned]
- [Close]
**Lead paragraph:** [first 3–4 sentences of the article]
**Companion note:** [how these two pieces work together in the content system]
```

## Quality checks
- [ ] LinkedIn and Substack versions use genuinely different angles
- [ ] LinkedIn post can stand alone without reading the Substack article
- [ ] Substack article adds real value beyond what's in the LinkedIn post
- [ ] Both versions are consistent with Lucía's positioning and voice
- [ ] Neither version fabricates metrics, outcomes, or tools not actually used
