# Skill: substack-writer

## Purpose
Produce complete Substack articles from real projects and insights. These are not LinkedIn posts made longer — they are a different format entirely: more depth, more structure, more pedagogy, more reflection.

## When to invoke
- When a project or lesson has enough substance for 800–1500 words
- When the audience needs to understand the *how*, not just the *that*
- When Lucía wants to build durable authority, not just visibility
- Scheduled cadence: 1x/2 weeks (Fase 1–2), 1x/week (Fase 3+)

## Core distinction vs LinkedIn
| LinkedIn | Substack |
|----------|----------|
| Discovery engine | Authority engine |
| Hook + observation + CTA | Intro + structure + depth + takeaway |
| ~150–250 words | ~800–1500 words |
| Gets people to notice | Gets people to trust |
| "This is interesting" | "This is useful / I learned something" |

The same project generates both: LinkedIn post brings people in, Substack article delivers the substance.

## Article structure

### 1. Title
- Specific, descriptive, useful
- Not clever-for-clever's-sake
- Should tell the reader exactly what they'll learn
- Examples:
  - "Cómo construí una app de reconciliación sin saber programar"
  - "El stack mínimo viable para automatizar AP en 2026"
  - "Por qué documento el proceso antes de automatizarlo"

### 2. Opening (3–5 sentences)
- Situate the problem — what was happening before
- Make the frustration or gap real and specific
- Do NOT start with "En este artículo voy a..."
- Do NOT start with a rhetorical question
- Start with the situation: "Tenía tres tabs de Excel abiertas..."

### 3. Body — structured sections
Each article should have 3–5 named sections. Options:
- **Problem → Attempt → Solution** (project narrative)
- **Step by step** (tutorial or guide format)
- **Before vs After** (transformation story)
- **Framework or mental model** (conceptual piece)

Each section: 150–300 words. Concrete. No filler. No "as we saw above."

### 4. What I actually learned
One section dedicated to the non-obvious takeaway.
Not "automation is great" — something specific:
- "El mayor error no fue técnico, fue no documentar el proceso primero"
- "Make me pareció más frágil que un script porque no entendía qué pasaba dentro"

### 5. Closing — actionable finish
Not "espero que esto te haya sido útil."
Options:
- What to do next if you're in the same situation
- A question that opens a conversation
- A direct pointer to the tool, repo, or resource mentioned

### 6. Nota del autor (required)
Brief closing note (3–5 sentences) explaining:
- Why Lucía wrote this specifically
- What in her real work motivated it
- What she hopes the reader does differently after reading

## Tone guidelines
- More reflective than LinkedIn but never self-indulgent
- First person but not navel-gazing — always grounded in the reader's utility
- Can use "I" more freely, show process and uncertainty
- Technical when needed, but explain the concept before the tool
- No motivational endings ("¡Vos también podés hacerlo!")

## Output format

```
---
TITLE: [article title]
SUBSTACK CATEGORY: [AP automation / tools / process design / career]
ESTIMATED READ TIME: [X min]
COMPANION LINKEDIN POST: [see below]
---

[Full article text]

---

NOTA DEL AUTOR
[3–5 sentences]

---

COMPANION LINKEDIN POST
[Short LinkedIn post that points to the article — different angle, strong hook, ends with "Artículo completo en Substack."]
```

## Quality checklist before output
- [ ] Title is specific and useful, not vague
- [ ] Opening situates the problem without a rhetorical question
- [ ] Each section has a clear purpose
- [ ] "What I learned" section contains a non-obvious insight
- [ ] No generic motivational endings
- [ ] Nota del autor is personal and honest
- [ ] LinkedIn companion post is a different angle, not a summary
